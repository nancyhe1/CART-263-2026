Nancy He
Response to: Halwaylism by  Alexandre Chardon, Emmett Walthers, and Kai Maquivar Lavers
https://crooksnwhiskey.github.io/3Dproject/

My experience interacting with Halwaylism, what stood out to me the most was how immersive and unsettling the experience felt, even though the environment itself is relatively simple. I didn’t expect a hallway space to feel so tense, but the combination of movement, sound, and perspective made it feel like I was constantly being watched. That feeling was probably the most compelling part of the project for me. A big reason for that is the use of the first-person perspective. Moving through the space from this point of view made everything feel more immediate and personal. I couldn’t see what was behind or around me unless I physically turned, which made me more aware of the unknown. From a technical side, this is likely done using a camera attached to player controls (like pointer lock controls in Three.js), and it works really well in supporting the concept. It’s not just a design choice, it directly affects how the user experiences the theme of surveillance.

The sound design also played a big role in shaping the experience. The audio was eerie but not overwhelming, which made it easy to stay immersed. It created a constant sense of unease in the background. This was likely done using looping audio and careful volume control, possibly with spatial audio features so that sound feels connected to movement in the scene. One moment I found especially interesting was reaching what felt like the exit door, only to be brought back to the beginning. I really liked this design choice because it strongly reinforces the project’s concept. It creates a sense that escape is impossible, and that the player is trapped in a loop of being observed. Conceptually, this ties very well into the idea of constant surveillance—no matter how far you progress, you are still within the system. It was surprising at first, but in a good way, because it made the experience feel more intentional and meaningful.

One part I found a bit confusing at first was the gameplay mechanic with the clickable squares. When I clicked the first one, I didn’t realize it had unlocked the next level, so I ended up stuck in the same area for a while. It wasn’t very clear that this was an escape-room style experience where you need to find hidden triggers to move forward. Once I figured that out, though, it became a lot more engaging. I think adding clearer feedback like a sound cue would make this part easier to understand. After watching the artist statement, I understood that the buttons were not fully random but selected from a set of three fixed positions, with the code using a random number to choose which of those positions would be active. I found this approach really interesting from a technical perspective, because it balances structure and unpredictability. It ensures that the buttons are always placed in intentional, designed locations while still making each playthrough feel slightly different.

Overall, I think Halwaylism does a really good job of combining technical elements with a strong concept. The use of Three.js, first-person controls, and simple but effective logic like loops and controlled randomization all work together to create a unique experience. Even though there were some small usability issues, the project was still engaging and memorable, especially because of how it made me feel while navigating the space.
